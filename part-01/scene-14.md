# Scene Fourteen

The ship changed its light at 03:12.

Kess Farec knew because she was still awake, and because over three years aboard this vessel she had learned the difference between its idle bioluminescence and the rarer shifts that meant the ship was doing something for reasons of its own. Most nights the walls moved through amber and blue-green without pattern she could map. Tonight the light narrowed.

Not brighter. More selective.

The cabin's curved walls dimmed to a low marine blue. One section by the console held amber — a vertical band, the exact width of a human body, running from floor to ceiling like a mark on the hull from the inside.

Kess did not stand immediately.

The distribution notice for Packet 31 was still open on the console where she had left it two days ago. Standard relay chain. Eleven days. She had not refreshed it. The number remained there like an old lie that had once been accurate. She had read the notice often enough that the words no longer carried information; they carried pressure. Time measured not in hours but in how long a person with her temperament could remain still before stillness became a form of decay.

She rose from the captain's chair and crossed the cabin.

"You finally decided to be interesting," she said.

The amber line remained where it was.

She touched the wall.

Warm. Not the ambient living warmth the hull always held, but something more focused — as if the ship had been using this section of wall for work. The hull did not answer her touch with movement. It held the heat and kept it.

Kess had stolen the vessel three years ago from men who had stolen it badly. Pirates, technically, though the word had flattered them. They had understood the ship the way children understand a blade: by the fact that it cuts. They had patched human interfaces over alien surfaces, welded hooks into structural ribs, painted over seams where light leaked through the hull because the leaking unsettled them.

Kess had killed one, bought off two, and inherited the rest.

She had then spent three years making the ship habitable without ever pretending it was hers.

That had been the arrangement between them. She would sleep in its rooms, tape her star charts over its unresponsive surfaces, wedge a folded jacket into a captain's chair built for a longer spine. It would carry her. She would not ask what it remembered from before human hands touched it. It would not answer questions she did not know how to phrase.

Tonight, for reasons she did not yet trust, the arrangement had changed.

She pulled up the relay route for Packet 31.

The display was Chilin-standard because the console module she had wired into the ship's systems two years ago had come from a Chilin relay station and still thought in Chilin's visual grammar. Packet origin, relay progression, expected delivery chain. Three intermediate nodes. One probability range for delay. Nothing unusual.

The amber strip on the wall brightened.

Kess looked from the console to the hull and back again.

"No," she said. "If you're going to do this, be less subtle."

The line on the screen shifted.

Not the route itself. The timing model. One node dimmed. Another brightened. The probability range changed — not enough to trigger an alert, not enough to count as revised data in any system built for procedure, but enough for a person who lived by edge conditions to see that the packet's path had narrowed.

Her first thought was Chilin manipulation. Her second was that the ship was using her own stolen module to tell her something that module had not been designed to show.

Her third thought arrived as memory.

Seven months ago, in the trading post on the lower level of a station she would not revisit, the old man had said: "The most expensive thing they've handled, they haven't sold yet. They don't know they have it."

At the time she had heard what she wanted — opportunity, rarity, asymmetry. A thing the brokers did not value correctly. A gap in the market large enough for a patient predator to fit through.

She heard something else now.

*They don't know they have it.*

Chilin moved knowledge as though knowledge remained itself while moving. Packet in, packet out. Price, queue, relay chain. The old man had not sounded like someone describing cargo. He had sounded like someone describing custody. As if the expensive part was not ownership, but what happened near it.

Kess leaned closer to the screen.

The altered timing model remained. A minor degradation in one relay branch. A faster probability curve through another. Kess opened the branch data, then the archived throughput she had been building quietly for months. The numbers did not match the standard routing assumptions. One node was carrying less than it should. Another was receiving more priority correction than the public chain showed. Not certainty. A discrepancy with direction.

If the ship had found the discrepancy first, it had still handed her something she could test.

She smiled without humor.

"You're telling me to move."

The ship's hum changed.

Not lower, as it had two days ago when she placed the order. More articulated. A pattern inside the tone, repeating just slowly enough that she almost missed it the first time. She held still. The pattern returned. Three pulses, then space. Three pulses, then space.

Not language. Worse. A regularity that invited interpretation.

Kess had lived long enough around traps to know the dangerous ones never looked like traps. They looked like recognition. A door that opened because you had finally found the right key. A system that appeared to confirm what you already wanted.

She did not trust the ship. She trusted it less, in some ways, than she trusted the old man. The old man had been human enough to lie in forms she knew. This vessel had architecture instead of expressions. Curves instead of eyes. Light instead of speech.

And yet the dependence was already there. Shelter, propulsion, concealment, the quiet fact that she had spent three years sleeping inside another civilization's solved problems and calling that independence.

She sat on the edge of the bunk and looked at the amber line in the wall.

The room held its living light. The taped star charts at her left curled slightly at the corners where adhesive had aged. The bottle on the console was still half-empty. Three jackets hung from the structural rib she used as a hook. Human disorder over alien coherence. The same room as two days ago. The same woman.

Not the same waiting.

The console still said eleven days. That estimate had been useful when she believed this was a purchase. Purchases rewarded patience. Hunting rewarded movement.

She opened the route map to full scale and began calculating intercept windows.

Not Tresil. Too visible once a Chilin carrier had docked. Not the final relay branch either; too many buyers watching the same chain by then, too many chances for a pattern to emerge. The second node was wrong in a way she liked — under-trafficked, technically routine, the kind of place where a delay of six hours would be written up as weather or hardware fatigue and filed forever under standard variance.

The old man had frightened her only once. Not when he described the principle. Not when he said others would try to own it. At the end, when she told him she was not afraid, and he answered, "That is what frightens me."

For seven months she had treated that as an old man's reflex: age mistaking appetite for danger because appetite no longer lived in his body.

Tonight she understood the sentence differently.

Not accusation. Diagnosis.

Fear was not what she lacked. She lacked stillness before the thing she wanted. The willingness to let an object remain partly unknown before reaching for control. That absence had built half her life. It had also kept her alive.

The route map settled into three possible crossings. Kess dismissed two. One required exposing her forged identity to a relay audit. Another depended on a fuel margin she did not trust from this far out. She left the third open: under-trafficked, ordinary, the kind of branch where small violence could hide inside routine.

The ship dimmed the rest of the cabin and kept the amber line bright.

If she was wrong, she would lose six days, burn reserve she might need later, and tell the ship something about how easily it could steer her.

Kess looked at the route again. Then at the wall.

"Fine," she said. "We move. But don't mistake that for obedience."

She entered the course correction manually.

The console accepted it. The ship accepted it a fraction of a second later — not with acceleration yet, not with any dramatic lurch, just a nearly imperceptible realignment in the hull's internal balance. A vessel preparing before motion. A body deciding which way it would fall.

Kess stood and placed her hand on the amber-lit wall again.

This time the warmth spread under her palm in a slow widening curve. Not reply. Not exactly. More like the ship had registered the new course and was already adjusting around it.

She thought of the sage. The packet. The false civilization she had built to get onto the buyer list. The eleven packets she had bought for cover and never read. The years the builders of this vessel had spent making a machine that remembered more than it displayed. Pieces that had sat separate long enough to become a life. Now, for the first time, they aligned.

Confidence remained. So did fatigue. Waiting had weight now. The scale of what moved through Chilin's network had begun to press back against her in the shape of time older than any market and older, perhaps, than theft.

She was still going to take Packet 31.

But the shape of taking had changed.

Outside the viewport, raw starlight held its distances. Inside the cabin, the ship's living glow settled into blue-green and amber. Kess stood in the middle of a room she had made hers and knew, with the precision she trusted most, that this was no longer true in the simple way she had meant it.

The packet had not arrived.

The waiting had ended.
