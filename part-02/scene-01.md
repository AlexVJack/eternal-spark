# Scene One

Cross Dar Lon left the western outpost twenty minutes after the storm warning had gone local and twelve minutes after he logged himself in the archive annex where he was supposed to remain until evening.

The outpost supervisor believed in visible compliance. Cross had learned that on the third day, after watching the man check sealed partitions by looking only at the indicator lights, satisfied that green meant intact and red meant problem and anything unlit could wait for quarter-end review. The method suited the corporation.

Tidalward had held the designation **stable site** for one hundred and twelve years.

Cross had been on the planet for nineteen days and no longer trusted the phrase enough to repeat it even in his own head.

Officially he was here to audit old archive partitions, reconcile surface beacons with orbital maps, and flag anything recoverable from pre-collapse infrastructure. The assignment was ordinary. Six weeks on a dead world with enough historical residue to justify a maintenance budget and too little living value to attract serious oversight.

He had not asked for Tidalward by name. He had only been saying yes to postings no one ambitious wanted, one after another, until the pattern looked accidental from the outside. Remote stations. Thin crews. Old files. Worlds where other people finished their shifts and returned indoors. Worlds where a person with enough patience could go on looking after the official work was done.

He told himself that was discipline.

Sometimes it was hunger wearing a calmer word.

The path to the coast began behind the weather tower, where corporation paving gave out against older stone. Rain had passed in bands all morning without ever clearing. By the time Cross reached the first exposed rise, his jacket had darkened through at the shoulders and the wind off the western sea had found the seam at his throat. He kept walking.

He had found the place twice from above and once from a lower corridor half collapsed into the cliff, but each time the weather or the work shift had forced him back before he could stay. Today he had left early enough to break both rules at once: the storm advisory and evening presence check. If he misjudged the light, the outpost would log the lie before he returned. The supervisor would make a note. Notes accumulated. Cross knew that too. He kept walking.

The sea appeared first as sound.

Then the coast opened beneath him: terraces of black stone, broken arches, long stairs descending toward ledges that the water reached in heavy intervals. The corporation's survey index called the site an obsolete ceremonial complex with associated remnant encampment structures and substrate failure across the inland chamber line.

Obsolete. Ceremonial. Remnant. Failure.

Below the largest arch, the old camp still held its shape in a way ruin rarely did by accident. Fire basins placed where heat could spread without choking the paths. Anchor cuts in the stone for fabric shelters. Cistern mouths where runoff gathered cleanly. Whoever had stayed here after the great transition had known exactly how much world they wished to keep around them and how much they preferred to leave unused.

That choice unsettled him more than any sign of collapse would have.

He went down the stair beneath the first arch and paused where the sea became visible through the interior openings. This had not been built against weather. Weather had been admitted into the design.

Cross touched the stone at shoulder height. Cold surface. Finer warmth underneath, held deep. The wall had been taking and returning temperature for longer than any corporation alloy would have survived here.

He moved on.

The outer passages narrowed into a darker interior where the sound of the sea arrived delayed and altered. Alcoves ran along the walls at measured intervals. Some were empty. In others he found bowls fused with pale mineral ash, rings cut into the floor where weight had stood for years, blackened stems of metal that had not rusted so much as dulled. He crouched once to photograph a shelf-mark and stopped before triggering the scanner.

The device would record location, time, local environmental spread, and upload all of it to the outpost cache when he returned.

He slid it back into his pocket.

He had told himself he was here because he wanted to understand what had happened on Tidalward. That was true. It was also true that understanding had begun to feel less shareable than the job required. The corporation liked findings best when they reduced a place. Cross had spent enough years inside that grammar to know what it did to whatever entered it first.

The fissure lay ahead where the corridor widened and the floor refused to remain one floor.

He crossed the threshold and stopped.

The chamber had split clean through. Not a crack across a room. A division. The darkness between the two sides was narrow enough in places to jump and deep enough a step later to make the idea stupid. Cross came nearer and knelt, one hand on the stone, the other reaching into his bag for the field lamp.

The beam went down and did not return in any simple shape.

It struck angled planes first. Then another set below them. Then an inward descent of exposed surfaces carrying a repetition too regular to call random once he had seen it twice. He shifted the light left. Right. The same dense bands answered from both sides of the split, deviating slightly, then repeating again lower down. Not carved channels. Not raw geology either. Something structural had lived inside the stone before the break made it visible.

Cross leaned farther than was sensible.

For a second the wet sole of his left boot slipped on grit. His free hand struck the floor hard enough to jar the lamp and send the beam skidding wild across the depth. He froze, weight half-forward, pulse suddenly high in his throat.

Pebbles clicked down the cut. He never heard them land.

He waited there until his breathing stopped sounding external to him, then sat back on his heels and looked into the fissure again with more anger than caution.

Not at the place. At himself.

There was always a moment like this once he got close enough to something real: the impulse to take one step too far, to wrench an answer out before it had agreed to exist.

He lowered the lamp again, steadier now.

The pattern remained. Not generous. Not hidden either. Exposed by violence, or by pressure, or by whatever Tidalward had once done to its own depths. He could not tell which. The corporation report called it substrate failure.

A small flame held to his right.

He had taken it for a reflection at first. Up close it was only a candle in a side alcove: narrow, pale, almost modest in shape. The flame did not flicker though the chamber was not sealed. Wax had spread around the base in generations of cooled spill, layer over layer, until shelf and residue had become one thing.

Cross stood and went to it.

There were others once he looked properly. One farther down the wall. One across the split. Two more above a side stair. The chamber did not brighten so much as reveal a rough order of witness. He switched off the lamp.

Darkness did not follow. The candles held enough of the room to change its scale.

He stayed still long enough for his eyes to adjust. He had no interest in arriving late to a place and calling it sacred just because he lacked the words for its engineering. The corporation made one kind of simplification. Visitors made another.

Still, the two rooms belonged together. The exposed depth. The flames arranged along it. Whatever this had been, it had not been left behind carelessly.

There was a larger light deeper in.

Cross took the stair beyond the fissure and followed it into the inner hall.

The room opened by degrees: side arches first, then height, then the full descent of steps toward a central basin broad enough to read as public architecture before anything else. Fire rose from it in a steady vertical body. No smoke. No visible fuel. Around it the candles multiplied across shelves, broken ledges, wall cuts, stair mouths, every surface that could hold a small decision and repeat it over generations.

The hall had once been made for many people. That fact remained in the proportions even now, after weather and emptiness had taken most other signs of use away. What remained was not grandeur exactly. More like durability given a social form.

Cross went down the first steps and stopped halfway.

Heat reached him in slow intervals from the basin. Behind him the storm pressed at the outer arches. Water sounded somewhere higher in the stone, finding old channels and still obeying them. The fire did not move like ordinary fuel. It held itself with a steadiness that made him think, unwillingly, of continuance beyond habit.

He disliked the thought for the same reason he had disliked sacred. It let language do the kneeling.

So he looked instead.

The walls nearest the basin were warm. Not hot. Kept. Less blackened than centuries of open burning should have made them. The older candles had sagged and fused into ridges so old they now altered the architecture.

He looked back toward the chamber with the fissure and then again at the basin.

The relation between them arrived without asking permission. Whatever had opened the stone had not ended here as simple ruin. It had been answered. Held to. Returned to long after the event itself had passed beyond living memory.

Cross tried, for a moment, to imagine the population that had once required a hall of this scale. Ports. Domestic quarters. Tens of thousands, perhaps more, moving through an ordinary continuity they had expected to keep. Then power enough to tempt them out of whatever had once restrained conflict here. Then the dead in numbers large enough that remembrance needed engineering.

He stopped there.

The room corrected him by refusing to become material for his feelings. He was not one of theirs. He had arrived centuries late, under corporate payroll, with wet boots and a falsified work log and the private wish that he might be the first person in a long time to understand what this world had hidden.

That wish was not noble enough to stand close to the fire without embarrassment.

He sat on the broad stair before the basin and let the embarrassment remain.

Above him the storm shifted. A brief thread of pale light reached through one of the higher arches and crossed the far wall, catching on something metallic set low into the stone beyond the basin. Cross saw it only because it answered the daylight differently from everything around it.

He stood.

The object was no larger than the first joint of his thumb: a fastening ring or sleeve driven into the mortar line behind one cluster of candles. Not ancient metal. Too regular. He crouched and cleared wax carefully away with the tip of his field knife.

His hand stopped.

The sleeve had once held something narrow in place against the wall. A rod, a marker, a tag. Whatever it had fixed there was gone. Under the wax, almost covered completely, a scratched index mark remained beside it: three short lines and a hooked fourth, cut with a tool too hard and too impatient to belong to the older stonework.

Cross had seen something close to it three days ago on one of the locked partitions in the outpost warehouse, half-hidden under a maintenance repaint at knee height where no official inventory note would ever mention it.

Not because the place had become sinister. Because it had ceased to be solitary.

Someone from the corporation had been here after the long empty years. Not as part of survey protocol. Not in any way that entered the archive he had been sent to audit. Someone had fixed something against this wall, hidden it under candles, and later taken it back out.

Cross looked toward the outer chamber, toward the fissure, toward the storm light at the arches, measuring distance against the hour. If he stayed much longer, the outpost would note his absence before he could get ahead of it. If he went back now, he might still reach the warehouse before evening meal and check the partition mark before anyone else entered the inventory lane.

He felt the old double movement in himself then: caution and appetite. One warned him that this was the moment to report properly, re-enter procedure, let the thing widen slowly. The other answered, with a swiftness that made him distrust it, that procedure was exactly how a world became stable on paper while its real history stayed buried under inventory language.

Cross took one last look at the basin and the patient lines of flame around it.

He had come here because the place would not leave him alone after he first saw it from the western shelf. That had been true.

He would return because now the wound had acquired a second edge: not only what happened here long ago, but who had found a use for that history later and kept it out of the record.

Outside, the sea struck the cliff below the arches with a depth that arrived in the hall a few seconds late. Cross turned and began climbing back toward the fissure, faster than he had entered, the field lamp loose in one hand, the lie in the outpost log already changing shape in his mind.
